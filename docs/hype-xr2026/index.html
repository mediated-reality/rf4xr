Workshop on Hyperrealism in XR 2026 in the era of Radiance Fields

Co-located with IEEE VR 2026, Daegu, Korea – March XX, 2026

Description

Recent advances in computer vision, computer graphics, and generative artificial intelligence (AI), particularly techniques based on 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [1] and Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) [2], have made the creation and authoring of photorealistic 3D assets more accessible than ever before. While technical challenges remain, the XR ("X" for any kind of reality) community should not focus solely on reconstruction/generation accuracy or processing speed [3]. Instead, our attention must shift toward the human factors that shape how realism from these assets is perceived, interpreted, and experienced.

The Workshop on Hyperrealism in XR (Hype-XR) builds upon progress in reality capture, rendering, and generated realities (e.g. 3D world generation models such as World Lab Marble), yet goes beyond them to confront the epistemological and experiential challenges that arise when bringing captured and generated reality into XR. Our goal is not merely to reproduce the real world through real-time reconstruction and rendering, but to build upon these capabilities and ask deeper questions: When, how, for what purpose, and why should we bring photorealistic assets into XR? Or, more simply, does higher fidelity always equal better XR? And more importantly, what are the underlying ethical concerns we need to address as the line between virtual and physical realities becomes increasingly blurred?

References

  • [1] B. Kerbl, et al., "3D Gaussian Splatting for Real-time Radiance Field Rendering," ACM TOG, 42:1--14, 2023.
  • [2] B. Mildenhall, et al., "NeRF: Representing Scenes as Neural Radiance Fields for View Synthesis," Proc. ECCV, 2020.
  • [3] K. Li, et al., "Radiance fields in XR: A Survey on How Radiance Fields are Envisioned and Addressed for XR Research," IEEE TVCG, 2025.

Topics: Hype-XR is exploratory in nature. We welcome contributions from any domain that discusses (hyper)realism in XR—including reality capture, processing, rendering, display, generation, and perception of captured reality, as well as conceptual framing of related challenges. Accordingly, the workshop will revisit major topics in IEEE VR under the theme of hyperrealism, but is not limited to the following areas:

  • Foundations of hyperrealism for XR: Definitions, terminologies, evaluation metrics, and standardization.
  • Reality capture for XR: Camera systems, streaming, and volumetography (or reality capture best practices and guidance).
  • Rendering and visual hyperrealism for XR: Radiance fields (or 3D Gaussian splatting and neural radiance fields), high-performance rendering,
  • Displays for hyperrealism for XR: High-performance 3D displays.
  • Multisensory and cross-modal hyperrealism: Audio, haptics, smell, taste, and their pseudo sensations.
  • Hyperrealism for machines: Photorealistic datasets, simulation environments, and domain shift problems.
  • Behavioral hyperrealism: Animations, gaze, motion synthesis, and uncanny valley mitigation of virtual humans.
  • Human Cognition and perception aspects of experiencing hyperrealism: Conviction of reality, presence, embodiment, and plausibility.
  • Ethics and accessibility: Ethics concerns, inclusivity, deception issues, and psychological safety in hyperrealistic XR experiences.
  • Applications: Simulation, training, art, digital archives, and entertainment.
